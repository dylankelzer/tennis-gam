Godot Architecture, Enemy AI, and Rally Combat System for a Tennis Roguelike Deckbuilder
Executive summary
This report proposes a production-ready, data-driven architecture in Godot Engine 1 for a single-player, turn-based roguelike deckbuilder that expresses tennis through a rally-resolution combat model and Slay the Spire–style run structure. It emphasizes (a) separation of simulation from presentation, (b) Resources + JSON for authored content, (c) deterministic seeds for reproducible runs, and (d) scalable enemy AI using finite state machines + utility scoring, optionally organized as a behavior tree layer for readability and tooling.
Key recommendations:
• Godot version strategy: target Godot 4.6.1-stable (current maintenance release), and pin that version for a milestone while keeping a tested upgrade path, because Godot supports only the latest patch in a minor series. 2
•Projectstructure:buildapure“rulesengine”in RefCounted classesand Resource definitions, with Node/Scene layers only for UI, animations, and orchestration. Godot explicitly recommends considering non-Node alternatives for heavy systems and points out that nodes have real overhead at scale. 3
• Data-driven content: use Resource files ( .tres during development, .res on export) for editor-friendly authoring and type safety, and optionally generate/override via JSON for modding and balance iteration. 4
• Rally combat: each encounter is a single standard game (Love/15/30/40/Deuce/Advantage/Game) per ITF rules; bosses extend into Short Sets or No-Ad variants (both explicitly defined by the ITF) to increase drama without making fights interminable. 5
• Enemy AI: implement “intent-like” behavior (telegraphed patterns) with a state machine plus card utility scoring per decision, inspired by turn-based AI practice and the modularity goals highlighted in behavior-tree literature (origin popularized in Halo 2 6 AI work). 7
• Balancing workflow: instrument pick rates, win rates, and encounter difficulty progression— aligning with the widely cited “metrics + iteration” approach used by Mega Crit Games 8 for Slay the Spire 9 balance. 10

Rally-based combat system design
Tennis scoring foundation and encounter formats ThetennisscoringmodelisstandardizedbytheInternationalTennisFederation 40 :
• Standard game: Love, 15, 30, 40, Game; at 40–40 it’s Deuce; then Advantage; you must win two consecutive points after Deuce to win the game. 41
• Approved alternatives include No-Ad games and Short Sets (first to 4 games, with tie-break rules) for events that want shorter formats. 42
For player familiarity, the United States Tennis Association 43 provides a clear “how scoring works” summary (4 points to win a game; deuce/advantage logic). 44
Encounter formats (design decision)
• Normal fights: Standard single game (matches per ITF Rule 5). 41
• Elite fights: No-Ad single game (faster, higher variance). 42
• Boss fights: Short Set to 4 games (or best-of-3 games) for sustained tension without full-length sets.
42
Core battle loop: four phases per point
Each point is the “mini-combat” inside the larger game/encounter.
Phase A — Setup - Determine server (per game). - Apply between-point persistent effects (fatigue, crowd,
boss auras). - Draw starting hands (player + enemy) with a Serve/Return constraint.
Phase B — Rally turns - Turn-based alternating play until the point resolves. - Player turn: play shot/utility cards up to stamina budget. - Enemy turn: AI selects response cards.
Phase C — Resolution - Resolve accuracy checks, forced errors, and “winner” thresholds. - Award point; apply “Condition damage” or other across-run consequences.

Phase D — Between-point - Reset point-only statuses; keep persistent statuses. - Increment tennis score (15/30/40 etc). - Refill partial stamina based on Endurance recovery.
Rally Pressure Meter as the “combat HP” analogue
To make tennis feel like tennis (not reskinned HP combat), use two coupled meters:
•RallyPressure(RP):asignedmeter [-RP_MAX, +RP_MAX] representingwhocontrolstherally right now.
• +RP favorstheplayer; -RP favorstheenemy.
• When RP reaches thresholds, it triggers point resolution.
• Stability (STB): each side has point-specific stability, representing balance/positioning under
pressure; low stability increases error chance on difficult balls.
This satisfies your requirement for: - “advantage/rally meter” (RP) - “winning points via x damage threshold” (RP threshold is the damage threshold)
Suggested parameters - RP_MAX = 100 - WINNER_THRESHOLD = 100 (hit winner / force error) - ERROR_ZONE = 70+ (high-pressurezonewhereerrorratesclimb)

Stamina vs Endurance vs Condition
Use Stamina as the per-turn resource (Slay the Spire’s “Energy” analogue).
• Endurance sets the maximum stamina and between-turn refresh.
• Condition is the across-run health-like pool (damage taken across fights). This is optional but
strongly recommended to preserve deckbuilder pacing; without it, every fight is a binary win/lose with little attrition tuning.

To preserve turn-based clarity:
• Card effects apply immediately, but hit resolution occurs when a “Shot” card is played.
• Separate card categories:
• Shot: changes RP, ball state, position; may apply statuses.
• Footwork: modifies position and stability; reduces incoming pressure next enemy turn.
• Focus: draw, discard, stance shifts; low/no pressure.
• Reaction (optional advanced layer): may be played during opponent turn if you have “Prepared” stacks.
Resolution for a Shot card:
1. Validate prerequisites (position, ball height). 2. Pay stamina cost.
3. Compute effective accuracy:
4. Base accuracy
5. ± modifiers from statuses (Fatigue, OffBalance, LowBounce, etc.) 6. ± matchup modifiers (e.g., slice into high ball)
7. Roll RNG (seeded deterministically).
8. If hit succeeds:
9.Apply RP += pressure_delta
10. Update ball state/spin for opponent
11. Apply statuses
12. If miss (unforced error):
13. Immediate point loss unless a “Let/Replay” mechanic is present (optional boss gimmick).
Status effects tuned for tennis
A compact status suite that produces tactical richness:
• Fatigue (stacking): -accuracy , -stability , increases stamina costs of Power shots • LowBounce: enemy power shots cost +1 stamina; volleys gain accuracy
• HighBounce: smash enabled; slice less effective
• OffBalance: next shot accuracy penalty; footwork cards remove
• Wide: if you don’t play Footwork this turn, lose stability
• NetPressure: passing shots gain pressure; lobs gain accuracy • ReadServe: next enemy serve loses pressure and/or accuracy • Confidence: small RP bonus when playing upgraded shots

Boss extensions: resting and “points over time”
To make bosses feel like bosses, give them match-long powers that change the cadence:
• Rest action: boss can choose “Changeover” once per game, spending its turn to recover stamina and purge Fatigue, but it concedes some RP (player gains momentum).
• Passive point pressure: boss gains automatic RP drift each rally turn unless the player plays tactical counters (e.g., “Slow the Pace”, “Deep Center Ball”).
• Between-game recovery (Short Set format): boss recovers more between games than normal enemies, pushing the player to end rallies decisively.

For turn-based tactical AI, the goal is consistent telegraphing and readable design. A practical hybrid for this project:
• Finite State Machine (FSM) at the top:
• ServeMode , ReturnMode , Defend , Attack , NetPlay , Panic , CloseOut
• Utility scoring within a state:
• evaluate each playable card in hand
• choose the argmax subject to stamina, prerequisites, and “scripted boss patterns”
• Behavior-tree style composition (optional):
• express “priority selectors” like: If can win point now → do it; else if in danger → defend; else execute

Difficulty scaling

Integration, balancing, test plan, and implementation prompts Integration guidelines
Principle: simulation produces events, UI consumes them.
• Simulation executes as a transaction: apply player inputs → resolve → emit a compact event list ( CardPlayed , RPChanged , StatusApplied , PointEnded , ScoreChanged ).
• UI replays those events into animations. If you later support “fast mode,” you simply skip animations and apply end state.
Godot mechanics that support this well:
• Signals for event publishing and decoupling. 22
• Autoloads for limited “global” services, with caution per best practices. 47 • Resources for definitions and save-friendly typed data. 25
Balancing guidelines
Deckbuilders benefit from instrumentation and iteration. Mega Crit’s public material on Slay the Spire highlights heavy reliance on metrics-driven, data-driven development to manage balance and keep content viable. 10
Recommended metrics:
• Card pick rate at reward screens
• “Card appears in winning deck” rate
• Average Condition loss per encounter by act
• Average points per encounter (how often deuce occurs) • RP swing distribution (are winners too spiky?)
• AI error rates vs player error rates
